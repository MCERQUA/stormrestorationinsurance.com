Storm Damage Roofing Contractors

You respond to insurance claims for wind, hail, and storm-damaged roofs. Working on compromised decking and around insurance adjusters creates a unique liability profile that standard roofing GL may not address.

Hail Damage Specialists

You inspect and replace roofs after hail events, often working under time pressure across dozens of jobs. Your crews face elevated fall risk on surfaces that may have hidden structural compromise from the hail impact itself.

After flooding or roof failure, you handle interior water damage remediation, drywall, flooring, and contents. Interior restoration after storm events requires GL coverage for completed operations and damage to contents in the work area.

Multi-State Storm Chasers

You follow catastrophe events from market to market — Texas, Colorado, Tennessee, Oklahoma, Nebraska. Multi-state storm chasers need GL and workers' comp that travel with them, plus commercial auto for crew trucks crossing state lines.

Coverage Types

What Storm Restoration
Contractors Need to Be Covered

A full-stack insurance program designed specifically for the hazards of storm restoration work — from rooftop falls to crew vehicles traveling to the next hail market.

🛡️

General Liability — Roofing-Rated

Storm restoration GL is underwritten for the specific risk profile of hail and wind damage work — compromised decking, skylight hazards, and homeowner disputes after catastrophe events.

  • Roofing-rated class code underwriting
  • Completed operations coverage (post-job claims)
  • Property damage during restoration work
  • Homeowner disputes and workmanship claims
  • Additional insured for adjusters and GCs
  • Products and completed operations aggregate
👷

Workers' Comp — NCCI Class 5551

Roofing is one of the most hazardous trades. NCCI Class 5551 workers' comp for storm restoration crews covers the severe injuries that result from falls on storm-damaged, unstable roof surfaces.

  • NCCI Class Code 5551 roofing specialists
  • Fall injuries on hail-damaged structures
  • Medical treatment and lost wage replacement
  • Multi-state workforce coverage
  • Seasonal and storm-chaser crews
  • Carriers willing to write prior claim history
🚛

Commercial Auto — Multi-State

Storm restoration crews drive hundreds of miles to reach active storm markets. Your fleet of trucks, trailers, and crew vehicles needs commercial auto coverage rated for interstate operations.

  • Company-owned trucks and trailers
  • Hired and non-owned auto (HNOA)
  • Interstate multi-state driving coverage
  • Cargo coverage for tools and equipment
  • Employee-driven personal vehicles for business
  • Coverage for crew transport vehicles
🔧

Tools & Equipment Insurance

Storm restoration crews carry significant equipment value — nail guns, compressors, ladders, safety harnesses, and specialty tools that may be left at storm sites overnight or transported across state lines.

  • Roofing tools and pneumatic equipment
  • Ladders and staging equipment
  • Safety harnesses and fall protection gear
  • Coverage at job sites and in transit
  • Theft from job sites during storm events
  • Replacement cost for damaged equipment
📋

Surety Bond

Many states require contractor license bonds before you can legally perform roofing work. During storm season, you may need to bond quickly in new markets as you chase catastrophe events across state lines.

  • Contractor license bonds (all 50 states)
  • Residential roofing registration bonds
  • Performance and payment bonds
  • Same-day or next-day bond issuance
  • Multi-state bonding during storm season
  • Texas, Florida, Colorado specialty bonds
☂️

Umbrella / Excess Liability

A single catastrophic fall injury or a multi-home damage claim during storm season can exceed your primary GL limits. Umbrella coverage provides an additional layer of protection above your underlying policies.

  • $1M–$10M additional coverage above GL
  • Follows form over GL and auto policies
  • Critical for larger restoration contracts
  • Required by many commercial property clients
  • Protection against catastrophic claim events
  • Cost-effective compared to raising underlying limits

💡 Example Claim

The Hail Season Job Gone Wrong — Texas

A storm restoration roofing contractor is working a large hail damage claim in the Dallas–Fort Worth area. A severe spring storm system has generated dozens of insurance restoration jobs. The crew is under pressure to complete as many roofs as possible before the market moves on.

On day three of an active week, a crew member steps through an unmarked skylight opening on a 3/12 pitch roof that the homeowner's adjuster had noted but failed to communicate to the roofing crew. The worker falls into the attic below — a drop of approximately 14 feet — and suffers a broken pelvis and two cracked vertebrae.

$185K Workers' Comp — Medical & Lost Wages
$22K GL — Homeowner Gutter Damage Claim
$207K Total Covered Loss

Workers' comp outcome: The crew member's $185,000 in medical treatment, surgical costs, rehabilitation, and 4 months of lost wages are covered by the workers' comp policy. The contractor pays the deductible and continues operations.

GL outcome: Separately, the homeowner files a claim asserting that the crew improperly re-fastened the gutters after replacing the roof. The gutters fell during a subsequent rain event and damaged a car parked in the driveway — $22,000 in vehicle damage. The GL completed operations coverage handles the claim and legal defense.

Without proper coverage: The contractor faces $185,000 in workers' comp liability out of pocket — likely bankrupting the business — plus a separate lawsuit for the vehicle damage. The fall victim could sue the contractor personally if no workers' comp was in place.

Storm restoration GL is underwritten specifically for contractors responding to catastrophe events — hail, wind, and flood — where properties may have weakened decking, skylight openings, and compromised structural integrity that increases fall risk and property damage exposure. Storm restoration GL typically uses roofing-rated class codes and may include catastrophe event endorsements. Getting the wrong policy classification can result in a coverage denial when you file a claim after a storm event. Always confirm your GL carrier knows you're doing insurance claim restoration work on storm-damaged structures, not standard re-roofs.

Most storm restoration roofing work falls under NCCI Class Code 5551 — Roofing — All Kinds. This is one of the highest-rated workers' comp codes because of the severe fall injury risk on rooftops. Storm restoration work on storm-damaged structures carries additional hazards: weakened sheathing, hail-impacted skylights, and wet or debris-covered surfaces. We work with carriers that write Class 5551 for storm restoration contractors and can bind coverage even for crews with prior workers' comp claims — something many standard carriers won't do. Properly classifying your crews under the right codes matters: misclassification can result in claim denials or unexpected audit surcharges at policy year-end.

Workers' comp policies are written with all-states endorsements or specific-states endorsements covering your primary operating states. Storm restoration crews typically operate a mix of company-owned trucks and employee-driven personal vehicles hauling equipment to job sites across multiple states. You need commercial auto for company-owned vehicles plus hired and non-owned auto (HNOA) coverage for employee-owned vehicles used for business. Surety bond requirements for storm restoration contractors vary by state and municipality. Many states require roofing contractors to carry a contractor license bond ranging from $5,000 to $25,000 before they can legally perform work. Some states like Texas require a specific registration or bond for residential roofing contractors doing insurance claim work. During storm season when you're entering new markets, you may need to obtain bonds in multiple states quickly — sometimes within 24–48 hours before the storm market moves on to the next city. We can issue contractor license bonds in most states same-day or next-day, which is critical when time is money and you're trying to get licensed and on the job before the opportunity passes.
Coverage gaps between storm seasons are a common and costly mistake for seasonal storm restoration contractors. If you let your GL or workers' comp lapse during the off-season and then try to rebind before the next storm season, you'll face higher premiums, tighter underwriting scrutiny, loss of any continuity credits you'd built up, and potential coverage gaps for incidents that occurred or were reported during the lapse period. We strongly recommend maintaining your core GL policy year-round — the annual cost is modest compared to the re-enrollment difficulty and potential late-discovered claim that falls in the gap. Workers' comp can sometimes be suspended during documented off-season periods with some carriers, but your GL should stay active. Talk to us before making any decision to let coverage lapse.
